Research

Several genetic targets have shown wide efficacy in improving the mammalian phenotype. One example is myostatin knockout, which has been successfully performed in mice1, rabbits2, sheep3, goats4, pigs5, dogs6, and monkeys7. It’s even been found naturally in humans8. In all of these mammals, myostatin knockout has resulted in greatly increased muscle mass.

However, myostatin knockout presents several challenges. In mice it’s been shown to weaken tendons9, 10, decrease exercise endurance11, and may not increase the muscle’s ability to produce force, despite increasing size12.

Another serious challenge for myostatin knockout is delivery. A high profile public announcement13 claimed to be the first to successfully inhibit myostatin in people. However, it’s unlikely that they’ve affected any significant portion of their muscle cells. In adult mice, 6-8 injections per limb of AAV-delivered CRISPR myostatin knockout successfully mutated only 5.8% of the targeted muscle cells14.
